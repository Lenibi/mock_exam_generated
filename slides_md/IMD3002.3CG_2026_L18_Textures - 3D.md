## Slide 6

Parallax Mapping
Implementation
Parallax Mapping is a relatively new method; introduced 
in 2001
It is implemented by displacing the texture coordinates at a point on the rendered polygon by a function of the view 
angle in tangent space (the angle relative to the surface 
normal) and the value of the height map at that point
At steeper view angles the texture coordinates are displaced more, thus giving the illusion of depth due to 
parallax effects

---

## Slide 7

Parallax Mapping
Silhouettes and Shadows
The method is normally a single step process that does not 
account for occlusion
Subsequent enhancements have been made to the method which incorporate iterative approaches to allow for 
occlusion and accurate silhouette rendering
Parallax Mapping is not yet common in games as it is 
comparatively slow due to the need for so many samples 
and conditionals for each pixel

---

## Slide 8

Relief Mapping
Overview
Relief Mapping is an alternative to parallax mapping 
that is much more accurate, and can support self -
shadowing and normal mapping
It involves offsetting texture coordinates towards the viewer, sampling a number of pixels in between, then 
drawing whichever one satisfies a certain height criteria

---

## Slide 9

Displacement Mapping
Introduction
Displacement Mapping is an alternative technique to 
bump mapping and its derivations (normal mapping and 
parallax mapping)
It uses a procedural texture or a height map to create the 
effect where the actual geometric position of points over 
the textured surface are displaced according to the value 
the texture function evaluates to each point on the surface

---

## Slide 11

Displacement Mapping
Introduction
It provides surfaces a great sense of depth and detail, 
permitting in particular self -occlusion, self -shadowing and 
silhouettes; on the other hand, it is the most costly of this class of techniques owing to the large amount of additional geometry
The key difference between displacement mapping and 
bump mapping is that with bump mapping the normal 
vector alone is perturbed –in displacement mapping the 
actual geometry is perturbed

## Slide 15

Displacement Mapping
Introduction
The main advantage in using displacement mapping is 
that it is a routine that can be performed on the graphics 
card (i.e. the GPU) based on a texture
Therefore, we can pass a simple geometry (i.e. relatively 
few polygons) and a texture height map and the GPU 
can displace the geometry accordingly
The advantage to this is that passing same geometry 
down a computer’s bus would be very slow as the 
geometry would be very large

---

## Slide 16

Displacement Mapping
Introduction
The other advantages to Displacement Mapping come in 
the obvious form of realism; with detailed geometry there 
is much greater realism
Compare the Bump Mapping techniques we have seen 
previously; we obtain problems with the silhouette of an 
object
At the edge of an object, even though it is supposed to be 
rough, because we didn’t perturb the surface (only the 
normal) it is still smooth (when it should be rough)

## Slide 30

Light Maps
2D Texture Light Maps
A 2D light map is a texture map applied to the surfaces 
of a scene, modulating the intensity of the surfaces to 
simulate the effects of a local light
If the surface is already textured, then applying the light 
map becomes a multi -pass operation, modulating the

---

## Slide 31

Light Maps
2D Texture Light Maps
A 2D light map can be generated analytically, creating a 
bright spot in luminance or colour values that drop off 
appropriately with increasing distance from the light 
center
As with other lighting equations, a quadratic drop off, modified with linear and constant terms can be used to 
simulate a variety of lights, depending on the area of the 
emitting source

---

## Slide 32

Light Maps

The main advantage of using 3D textures for light maps is

The textured light source can be positioned globally with 
the appropriate texture transformations then the scene is rendered

## Slide 39

Reflection Mapping
Method Overview
First the scene is rendered from the view point of the 
surface to be mapped
Next, this image is turned into a texture map, which is then mapped to the surface
Note that this only does a "one bounce" reflection, whereas true global illumination algorithms, such as ray 
tracing, will do multiple bounces 
A drawback of this is the absence of self reflections: we 
cannot see any part of the reflected object inside the 
reflection itself

---

## Slide 40

Reflection Mapping
Method Overview
There are several ways of storing the surrounding 
environment; the most common ones are:
Spherical Environment Mapping – where a single texture 
contains the image of the surrounding as reflected on a mirror 
ball; this is commonly known as “Standard Environment 
Mapping”
Cubic Environment Mapping –where the environment is 
unfolded onto the six faces of a cube and stored therefore as 
six square textures

---

## Slide 41

Reflection Mapping
Spherical Environment Mapping
This involves the use of a textured sphere at an infinite 
distance away from the object that reflects it
By creating a spherical texture using a fisheye lens, via pre-rendering, or using a light probe, the texture is 
mapped onto a hollow sphere, and the colours are calculated by using the light vectors from the points on the object to points on the environment map

---

## Slide 42

Reflection Mapping
Spherical Environment Mapping
This technique may produce results that are superficially 
similar to those produced by ray tracing, but incurs less of 
a performance hit because all of the colours of the points 
to be referenced are calculated beforehand
Therefore, all the renderer has to do is calculate the angles of incidence and reflection

---

## Slide 43

Reflection Mapping

There are a few key problems with Spherical Environment 
Mapping:
Due to the nature of the texture used for the map, there is an

Cubic Environment Mapping addresses at least this issue

## Slide 46

Reflection Mapping
Cube Mapping
Cube mapped reflection is done by determining the 
vector that the object is being viewed at
This camera ray is reflected about the surface normal of where the camera vector intersects the object
This results in the reflected ray which is then passed to the cube map to obtain the effective pixel value that the camera then sees as if it is on the surface of the object
This creates the effect of a reflective object
