## Slide 12

Shadows
Shadow Volumes
We can cast shadows onto arbitrary objects by using a 
stencil buffer; this technique is sometimes called 
“Volumetric Shadows ”
Imagine a point and a triangle; by extending lines from 
the point through the vertices of the triangle to infinity we 
obtain an infinite pyramid
The part under the triangle, i.e. the opposite side to the 
point, is a truncated infinite pyramid
The upper part is simply a pyramid

---

## Slide 13

Shadows
Shadow Volumes
Assume the point is a light source and therefore any part 
of an object which is inside the volume of the truncated 
pyramid is in shadow; this volume is called the “shadow 
volume”

---

## Slide 14

Shadows
Shadow Volumes
Now, for a scene we can follow a projected ray through a 
pixel until that ray hits the object to be displayed on the 
screen
While the ray is on its way to the object, we increment a 
counter each time it crosses a face of the shadow volume 
that is front facing (i.e. facing toward the camera)
Therefore, the counter is incremented each time the ray 
goes into shadow

---

## Slide 15

Shadows
Shadow Volumes
In the same manner, we decrement the same counter each 
time the ray crosses a back -facing side of the truncated 
pyramid
The ray is then going out of a shadow; the ray hits the object that is to be displayed at that pixel – if the counter 
is greater than zero, then that pixel is in shadow (otherwise it is not)
This principle works when there is more than one polygon that casts shadows

## Slide 18

Shadows
Shadow Maps
Shadow maps work using a Z -buffer based renderer
The main idea is to render the scene, using the Z- buffer 
algorithm, from the position of the light source that is to 
cast the shadows we are concerned with
For each pixel in the z -buffer, the z -depth now contains 
the distance to the object closest to the light source

---

## Slide 19

Shadows
Shadow Maps
We call the entire contents of the Z- buffer the “ Shadow 
Map”, also sometimes called the “ Shadow Depth Map”, 
or the “ Shadow Buffer ”
To use the shadow map, the scene is rendered from a 
second time, but this time from the perspective of the 
viewer
As each drawing primitive is being rendered, its location 
is compared to the shadow map; if a rendered point is 
farther away from the light source that the value in the 
shadow map, then that point is in shadow

---

## Slide 20

Shadows
Shadow Maps
If the point is the same as the point in the shadow map, 
then it is lit (or not in shadow)
Here we see the two rendered conditions, first from the light’s perspective, then from the eye’s perspective

---

## Slide 21

Shadows
Shadow Maps
It is important to remember that whilst we have to render 
two frames, we only have to do this  each time if the 
object (we are creating the shadow for) or the light is 
actually moving
Therefore, even if the camera is moving, there is no need to re-render for the light’s position as it will remain the 
same
It is also important to remember that even the properties of the light can change (and the material of the object) 
and the shadow map will remain the same

## Slide 24

Fog
Introduction
Fog effects are an important part of realistic computer 
graphics
Example: flight simulators often use fog effects to 
simulate adverse weather conditions - this allows air crews 
to be safely familiarized with difficult operational scenarios, such as landing on fog -obscured runways
To be realistic, fog effects must closely model the appearance and behavior of real fog conditions

---

## Slide 25

Fog
Introduction
This means that fog effects must be capable of modelling 
patchy or otherwise non -uniform fog or haze
Realistic fog effects must also be capable of animation; this allows fog to swirl or move in a manner that mimics 
natural fog
Several techniques have been developed that can be 
used within the stages of a graphics pipeline to create 
fog effects; these include: colour blending, texture 
mapping, and volumetric rendering

---

## Slide 26

Fog
Colour Blending
For colour blending, a fog colour is selected or defined 
(usually a shade of grey)
During the “fogging process”, the graphics pipeline blends the fog colour into each of the pixels within the image 
being fogged
The graphics pipeline determines the amount of fog 
colour to add to the image’s pixels by calculating a 
weighting factor for each pixel

---

## Slide 27

Fog
Colour Blending
Each pixel’s weighting factor is a linear or exponential 
function of the distance between the pixel and the eye-
point
The graphics pipeline blends the predefined fog colour 
into the each pixel in accordance with the pixel’s 
weighting factor

---

## Slide 28

Fog
Texture Mapping
For texture mapping, a series of texture maps are 
generated
Each texture map is configured to make objects appear as if they are being viewed through a specific fog depth
Thus, a first texture map might make objects appear as if they were obscured by one meter of fog and a second 
texture map might make objects appear as if they were 
obscured by ten meters of fog, etc.

---

## Slide 29

Fog
Texture Mapping
In some cases, the series of textures are encoded within a 
3D texture map
During the fogging process, the graphics pipeline selects the appropriate texture map for each primitive within the 
image being fogged
Selection of texture maps is based on the distance 
between the primitives and the eye
The pipeline then textures each primitive with the appropriate texture map

---

## Slide 30

Fog
Volumetric Rendering
With volumetric rendering, a 3D volume is generated to 
model the desired fog effect
During the fogging process, the graphics pipeline applies the 3D volume to the space between the primitives 
included in an image and the eye -point
Typically, this is accomplished through the use of a 
volumetric rendering technique such as ray- casting, voxel 
rendering, or 3D texture -slice composition

---

## Slide 31

Fog
Technique Comparison
Colour blending, texture mapping and volumetric 
rendering are all effective techniques for rendering uniform fog effects
However, these techniques are subject to disadvantages that can make them undesirable
For example, when using colour blending to create fog effects, the amount of fog colour is controlled strictly by pixel distance
This means that fog effects produced by these techniques tend to be uniform (non -patchy) and constant over time 
(inanimate)

---

## Slide 32

Fog
Technique Comparison
Texture mapping and volumetric rendering each allow for 
the creation of non -uniform (patchy) fog effects
For this reason both of these techniques offer a higher degree of realism than is possible for colour blending
Unfortunately, neither texture mapping nor volumetric rendering are able to provide animated fog effects

---

## Slide 33

Fog
Technique Comparison
Both of these techniques are also limited in other fashions
Texture mapping, fails to provide a mechanism that allows 
the appearance of a fog effect to change as the eye -
point moves in relation to an image
Volumetric rendering overcomes this difficulty but generally requires expensive hardware support within the
