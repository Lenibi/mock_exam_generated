## Slide 27

Skyboxes & Skydomes
´éĘHow it Works
´éĄAs a viewer moves through a 3D scene, it is common 
for the skybox to remain stationary with respect to 
the viewer
´éĄThis technique gives us the illusion of the environment 
being very far away since other objects in the scene 
appear to move, whilst the skybox does not
´éĄThis imitates the stereo effect we are used to: where 
distant objects (e.g. clouds, stars and mountains, etc) 
appear stationary when the viewpoint is changed by 
small distances

## Slide 29

Mipmapping
´éĘIntroduction
´éĄObjects in a scene are rendered at different 
distances (near and far) from the viewing camera, 
any objects with textures applied will also be 
handled in the same way
´éĄAs a textured object moves closer or further away from the camera, the textures must be filtered in 
order to be applied to the more distant objects
´éĄAs the filtering is based on distance -  flickering 
occurs as the camera moves, this is due to the decimation process

---

## Slide 30

Mipmapping
´éĘIntroduction
´éĄThe solution to this problem is mipmapping
´éĄMipmapping is a process where a set of filtered 
texture maps of decreasing resolution are 
generated from a single high resolution texture prior 
to use
´éĄWe use this to reduce flickering during texture mapping and display
´éĄEach individual mipmap is a distinct texture based on the original; it is a downsized and filtered version 
of the previous mipmap level

---

## Slide 31

Mipmapping
´éĘIntroduction
´éĄMipmapping allows the rendering system to apply the 
appropriate level of detail for a texture when rendering 
rather than shrinking the original texture
´éĄWe can apply different mipmaps to combine into a single 
texture; i.e. we are not constrained to using only one 
mipmap for a specific surface

Mipmapping
´éĘGenerating Mipmaps
´éĄEach mipmap level is generated by taking half the

## Slide 35

Bump Mapping
´éĘIntroduction
´éĄBump Mapping is a technique that is used to make a 
surface appear perturbed in some manner; bumpy, 
wrinkled, wavy, etc
´éĄThis results in is a more detailed surface 
representation that more closely resembles the 
details inherent in our natural world
´éĄWe use bump mapping to effectively simulate a 
surface that would utilise many polygons ÔÇô which is 
obviously inefficient for our rendering engine (i.e. more polygons = slower rendering speed)

---

## Slide 36

Bump Mapping
´éĘIntroduction
´éĄIn place of using a texture to change the colour 
component of a surface, we use it instead to perturb 
the surface normal
´éĄThe geometric normal (i.e. the perpendicular vector 
to the surface) remains the same, we merely modify 
the normal used in our lighting model (e.g. Phong)
´éĄIf we use the normal per vertex, we will have a 
smooth surface between polygons, but if we use 
normal per pixel, this is where we end up with the 
bumpy surface
